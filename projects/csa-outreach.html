CSA Satellite Program.

A free satellite program for at-risk youth, built in partnership with the Canadian Space Agency — and I wrote the $50K grant that funded it.

Program Design Grant Writing Aerospace Education CSA Partnership
Purpose

Why I built this.

I had access to real aerospace engineering experience through QSET. Most high school students — especially those from underprivileged backgrounds — don't. I wanted to change that for at least one group of kids, and I had the tools to do it: a relationship with the Canadian Space Agency, a connection to the Kingston Robotics Lab, and the ability to write a grant.

So I built the program. At-risk youth designing, wiring, coding, and launching actual satellites. Not simulations. Not kits. Real hardware, real launch, real data.

Process

How I built it.

Grant application, partnership formation, curriculum design, program delivery, and launch.
01

Grant writing and funding

Wrote the proposal that secured $50K from the Canadian Space Agency. The grant covered all program costs — components, tools, launch logistics — so participants paid nothing. Writing the proposal meant articulating the educational value, the partnership structure, and the technical feasibility of putting student-built hardware into the stratosphere.

02

Partnership with CSA and Kingston Robotics Lab

Established the formal partnership between Connections (Queen's engineering outreach), the Canadian Space Agency, and the Kingston Robotics Lab. Each partner brought something specific: CSA provided credibility and funding, Kingston Robotics Lab provided facilities and expertise, Connections provided the student pipeline and instructional framework.

03

Curriculum design

Designed the technical curriculum from scratch: embedded systems basics, sensor integration, circuit wiring and soldering, 3D CAD, and satellite structure design. The curriculum had to work for students with no prior engineering background and build to a working payload over the program duration.

04

Program delivery and satellite build

Ran the program with participants designing and assembling their own payload systems — writing code, wiring sensors, testing integration. Supported students through the debugging process; the goal was for them to own the technical decisions, not just assemble a kit.

05

Launch

Payload launched via stratospheric balloon. Student-built hardware went to the edge of the atmosphere and returned with data. For most participants, it was the first time something they built had ever physically left the ground.

Result

What I made.

The program ran successfully, culminated in a real launch, and established a model for ongoing STEM-space collaboration between QSET and Connections. The partnership continues, with future launches planned. The $50K grant covered everything — no cost to participants. The most important outcome wasn't the data the satellite collected. It was the students who saw their hardware come back from the stratosphere.
